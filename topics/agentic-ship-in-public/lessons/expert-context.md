# Ship in public — expert context

## What a portfolio proves

Hiring for agentic engineering has converged on a short, nameable signal set — the things a strong
reviewer actually reads a portfolio *for*, once the buzzwords are stripped out.

- **Real, self-designed projects over tutorial clones.** A tutorial reproduction proves you can follow
  steps; a project you scoped, designed, and finished proves you can *engineer*. Reviewers can tell the
  difference in about a minute, because a self-designed agent has decisions in it — and decisions are
  what they want to interrogate.
- **Architecture decisions you can defend.** The README's job is not to list features; it is to record
  *why* the agent is built the way it is — why a bounded loop, why these tools, why this validation
  boundary. This is the portfolio equivalent of an **architecture decision record**: one clear "we chose
  X over Y because Z" is worth more than a wall of code, because it shows judgment under tradeoffs.
- **An eval suite as proof of measurement.** Anyone can claim an agent "works." An eval suite — cases
  plus a judge plus a pass rate — proves you *measured* it and know where it fails. Shipping evals reads
  as senior precisely because most portfolios skip them; it signals you treat an agent as a system to
  verify, not a demo to hope over.

The through-line: a portfolio that gets you hired trades *listing* AI as a skill for *demonstrating* it —
a working agent, a README that defends its architecture, and evals that measure it. Knowing that this is
the signal — and building your capstone to send it deliberately — is what separates a candidate who
"knows about agents" from one a team can actually hire.
